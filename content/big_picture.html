Baseball has always been a numbers game. Whether it be batting average, earned run average, or wins above replacement, players’ in-game statistics dictate nearly every management decision, as small as a call to the bullpen and as big as releasing a player. Although these in-game statistics can be very telling on a player-by-player basis, they are not always indicative of a team’s success as a whole.

figure displaying disparity between highest win perc and highest HR/R/H

One of the most significant divisions between teams are they’re relative payrolls. The wealthiest teams in the league often can have as high as four times the funds of the poorest teams for any given season. Surely, an inequality gap this massive must have a noteworthy impact on teams’ success relative to one another; however, the influence of this resource gap is not as straightforward as one might think.

Our initial and obvious instinct was that teams’ winning percentage should increase as their payroll increases, and that certainly holds true, as shown in the figure below:

A team with even a two-fold increase of payroll should be expected to drastically outperform the lower spending teams. However, the above graph tells us that, on average, a $1 million increase in payroll can only be expected to result in a 0.06% in win percentage. That means that an extra $1 million cannot even buy a single win. In a 164-game season, 1 win makes a difference in win percentage of approximately 0.6%. Combined with the results of the graph above, 1 win would cost approximately $10 million dollars on account of payroll alone.

However the above plot also has a lot of noise. The team with the highest winning percentage between 2000 and 2016 had a payroll of less than $100 million. All this noise indicates a more nuanced pattern underlying teams’ relative success in the MLB.
