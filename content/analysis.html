Data Analysis Motivations

For our data analysis, we are most interested in the relationship between the winning percentage of each Major League Baseball team and each team’s payroll for a given season. We hope that looking at this relationship will provide insight into whether winning in the MLB is based on luck. We wish to look at the whether a particular team’s winning percentage changes each season with that team’s payroll. How does the payroll affect team performance? Where do the dollars from an increase in the payroll go? What factors lead to a team losing even with such high payrolls? The answers to these questions may differ between the MLB teams. Some teams may consistently perform better with higher payrolls while other teams may not, so we are interested in the variability of these answers among the MLB teams.

From these faceted plots, it is not consistent among all teams that as payroll increases, winning percentage also increases. For exactly half of the MLB teams, as payroll increases, their winning percentage for that year decreases. Those 15 teams that have a positive slope between their winning percentage and payroll for each season will be named “Increased Performance Teams” while those 15 teams with negative slopes we have named “Decreased Performance Teams.”

In our analyses that follow, we look at what factors influence this divide between a positive and negative relationship between payroll and winning percentage.

Positive Relationship

When analyzing the Increased Performance Teams, we wanted to further look at the relationships between payroll and certain game statistics for each season. We first looked at payroll and hits to see if an increase in the number of hits per season was influencing this increased winning percentage as payroll increased. To do this, we calculated the total hits for each team each year by summing their home hits and their away hits. We then joined this dataset with the payroll dataset and plotted the total hits of each team against their payroll for each year between 2000 and 2016.


To our surprise, for 11 out of the 15 teams, as payroll increases, the total number of hits in a season decreases. This suggests that even though teams are spending more money on more valuable players, their offensive performance in their total hits for a season does not increase.

We then looked at the relationships between payroll and homeruns for each season. It is possible that teams are spending more money on players known for hitting home runs and therefore bring in more runs rather than players who just hit and get on base without bringing in a run. We first calculated the total home runs for each team each year by summing their home home runs and their away home runs. We then joined this dataset with the payroll dataset and plotted the total homeruns of each team against their payroll for each year between 2000 and 2016.


Again, to our surprise, 7 out of he 15 teams produced fewer home runs as payroll increased. From these graphs on the relationships between payroll and hits and payroll and home runs, it appears as though the offensive performance does not improve. So what then are these teams spending money on to increase their winning percentage?

Rather than looking at offensive performance, we looked at defensive performance, particularly pitching. The first statistic we looked at for pitchers was strikeouts. In the baseball dataset, the variables h_strikeouts and v_strikeouts refer to the total number of times players struckout for the home team and visiting team respectively. To make this a statistic for the home team and visiting team pitchers, we reversed their variables. More explicitly, the total strikeouts for the home team pitchers is represented by the visiting team’s strikeouts (v_strikeouts) and the total strikeouts for the visiting team pitchers is represented by the home team’s strikeouts(h_strikeouts). We calculated the total strikeouts for each team for each year, joined this dataset with the payroll dataset, and plotted the total number of strikeouts for each team against their payroll for each year between 2000 and 2016.


These plots show that for 15 out of the 15 teams, as payroll increases, the total number of strikeouts performed by the pitchers of each team also increases. We then looked at the earned runs statistic to see if this change in pitcher performance was consistent with the increase in payroll. Similar to the strikeout variables, h_team_earned_runs and v_team_earned_runs are the earned runs for the players of the home team and visiting team respectively. Here again we represented the earned runs for the home team pitchers with v_team_earned_runs and represented the earned runs for the visiting team pitchers with h_team_earned_runs. We calculated the total earned runs for each team for each year, joined this dataset with the payroll dataset, and plotted the total earned runs for each team against their payroll for each year between 2000 and 2016.


According to the plots, as payroll increases, the total earned runs for 15 of the 15 teams also decreases. These faceted plots along with the plots for total strikeouts against payroll suggest that along with an increase in payroll, the performance of the pitching improves with more strikeouts and less earned runs against the pitchers.

Linear Regression

To conclude our analysis, we took all the variables that we previously observed (payroll, hits, homeruns, strikeouts, and earned runs against) and conducted a forward selection stepwise regression to see how well these variables can predict the response variable of winning percentage.

We used Mallow’s C~p to determine the number of predictors that provided the best overall model. Mallow’s C~p compares the full model to models with a subset of the predictors.The model with the smallest C~p statistic indicates the best overall model with the least amount of unexplained error. The following code is what we used to discover that the model with four of the five predictors was the best model in predicting winning percentage:

fwd <- leaps::regsubsets(win_perc ~ . - h_name - year, 
                         data = all,
                         method = "forward",
                         nvmax = 10)
which.min(summary(fwd)$cp)

Limitations

There are a few limitations in our analysis, specifically for the teams that have a negative relationship between payroll and winning percentage. We only analyzed two of the fifteen total teams that have that negative relationship, then generalized our findings. It could be true that what we find for those two teams is not the same for the other thirteen teams not analyzed. We also only did analysis of the pitchers for the teams with the negative relationship, and did not do analysis of other defensive positions, or any offensive factors.
